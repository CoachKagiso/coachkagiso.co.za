## Why a "blog" page in 2026 is the wrong frame (still true, with one correction)

Three things have changed since the era when "start a blog" was good marketing advice for service businesses.

**Generic SEO is a graveyard.** Google's AI-powered search summaries answer most informational queries directly without sending traffic to source pages. *"How to write a CV"* type articles ranked organically in 2018 and now rank for nothing because AI summaries replace them.

**But three categories of search still drive significant clicks.** This is the v1.0 correction. Transactional queries ("CV review south africa"), comparative queries ("career coaching prices south africa"), and local + specific queries ("career coach johannesburg") are all alive. AI Overviews don't fully serve them because the user wants to buy, compare, or find a local provider — not get a summary. Pillar pages targeting these queries still rank and convert.

**The newsletter ate the blog.** Direct subscriber relationships outperform algorithmic distribution. Modern "blogs" for solo creators are blog-and-newsletter hybrids: same content, two delivery surfaces.

**Series content beats standalone posts.** A reader who finds Episode 3 of a series will read Episodes 1 and 2 immediately and subscribe so they don't miss Episode 4. Series builds anticipation, retention, and subscriber growth in ways one-off articles can't.

The Insights page reflects all four insights. Series-driven for retention. Pillar-page driven for acquisition. Newsletter-integrated for compounding ownership. AI-resistant in form so the work isn't replaceable.

## Three principles for everything that ships under Insights

**1. Series + pillars + manifestos.** Each piece belongs to one of three production lanes: a running series (retention), a search pillar page (acquisition), or an occasional manifesto (brand POV). Standalone posts outside these lanes are rare.

**2. Frameworks before advice.** Generic advice ("update your LinkedIn") is what AI assistants now give for free. Memorable content gives frameworks: named patterns, archetypes, decision rules. Frameworks become language readers repeat to friends, which is how organic reach actually compounds in 2026.

**3. Pave, don't pitch.** No article on this page mentions the Saturday Masterclass directly. The masterclass is paved through three mechanisms: introducing her frameworks (which she develops further in the masterclass), naming problems without fully solving them (the deeper work happens in coaching), and ending each piece with a "where this goes next" link that routes to the right place in her funnel based on the post's topic.

## The new tier system — four production lanes

### Tier 0 — Search Pillar Pages (NEW in v1.1)

These are the acquisition engine. Long-form, definitive resources that target specific search queries, drive new visitors to the site, and feed the funnel. Without them, the brand-led series content has no audience to retain.

**Format:** 1,800-2,500 word definitive guides
**Cadence:** One per month (12 per year)
**Voice mode:** Tactical Teacher, with Reflective Leader moments
**Job in the funnel:** Convert search traffic to email subscribers and paid clients
**Internal links:** Every pillar links to at least one service page and 2-3 series articles

Each pillar page uses the same structure:
- Strong opener with a specific claim
- Problem framing (1-2 short sections)
- Her named framework (1 section)
- Step-by-step application (2-3 sections, scannable)
- Real example or case study (anonymised)
- Lead magnet CTA (free template, checklist, or downloadable)
- Soft service CTA (links to the matching service page)

Critical rule: pillar pages must be in Kagiso's voice (per the Brand Voice Guide), not generic SEO content. Specificity, opinion, named frameworks, and SA examples are non-negotiable. Generic SEO pieces don't rank in 2026 anyway — Google rewards information gain.

Brand-level POV pieces, one every 6-8 weeks. Highest shares, lowest direct conversion. Brand investment.

## Internal linking — the rule

Every article links to at least one service page. Every service page references at least one article.

This is free authority transfer that compounds over time. The SEO audit flagged this as a gap in v1.0 of the strategy. v1.1 fixes it.

**Specific link patterns:**

- Every Visibility Audit article links to LinkedIn Optimisation Package (R300) or CV Revamp (R400)
- Every Promotion Files case study links to Career Clarity Session (R800)
- Every pillar page links to the matching service AND to 2-3 related series articles
- Every manifesto piece links to the next manifesto OR to the most-relevant pillar page
- Every lead magnet thank-you page links to the matching pillar article

Service pages link back: the CV Revamp page links to "The ATS Test Your CV Is Failing in SA"; the LinkedIn Package page links to the LinkedIn Optimisation pillar; Career Clarity links to two Promotion Files case studies.

## Production model — AI drafts, Kagiso polishes

This is the v1.1 production reality. Articles are drafted by AI (using this document, the Brand Voice Guide, the Service Architecture, and the SEO audit as references). Kagiso reviews and polishes for voice, accuracy, and any factual details only she knows. Then they ship.

**Process per article:**

1. AI drafts article using the Brand Voice Guide and humanizer rules. Includes target keyword (for pillar pages), framework name, real example placeholders, internal link map, and lead magnet CTA where relevant.
2. Kagiso reads the draft. Replaces placeholder examples with real anonymised cases from her practice. Adjusts any phrasing that doesn't sound like her. Confirms the framework name is one she'd actually use.
3. Article gets paste-ready copy plus the meta title, meta description, OG image direction, and internal link list.
4. Builder (Cursor, Claude Code) puts it on the site.

**Time per article (Kagiso's involvement):**

- Visibility Audit (600-800 words): 20-30 minutes review
- Promotion Files (800-1,200 words): 30-45 minutes review (longer because she's adding her real client examples)
- Manifesto (800-1,200 words): 30-45 minutes review
- Pillar page (1,800-2,500 words): 45-60 minutes review

A 12-piece quarter requires roughly 6-8 hours of Kagiso's time across the quarter. Achievable for a side-hustle solo coach.

## "What NOT to write" — sharpened in v1.1

The v1.0 list overshot. There's a difference between generic AI-replaceable listicles and opinionated SEO pieces with named frameworks.

**Don't write (still bans):**
- "10 tips for [anything]" (no specific framework, no opinion)
- Generic motivational content
- Listicles aggregating other people's content
- Posts that mention her current employer, AIISA, banking, insurance, financial services
- Pieces referencing specific company names without permission
- Anything in wellness/manifesting/abundance territory
- Generic "how to" content with no SA context, no opinion, no named framework

**DO write (the v1.1 distinction):**
- Opinionated pieces with named frameworks, even if the underlying topic is "common"
- SA-specific takes on universal topics
- Long-form definitive guides that target search queries
- Pieces with specific data, real examples, and clear position-taking
- Lead-magnet support content that drives a downloadable asset

The pillar test: *"Would this still be valuable if every other career coach in SA also wrote the same article tomorrow?"* If no, skip it. If yes, ship it — even if the topic looks generic on the surface, the substance and voice make it irreplaceable.

**The "solve surface, reveal deeper" rule (NEW in v1.2):** Every pillar page and tactical article should fix the immediate problem AND reveal the strategic problem behind it. Two examples:

- A CV pillar page fixes the formatting issue (the surface problem) AND reveals that the reader's CV is failing because their career story is unclear (the strategic problem). The fix delivers value. The reveal creates the booking.
- A LinkedIn audit fixes the headline (the surface problem) AND reveals that visibility is a system, not a one-time edit (the strategic problem). The fix gets the download. The reveal makes the reader think about Career Clarity.

Articles that only solve the surface problem teach the reader to do it themselves and lose them. Articles that solve the surface AND reveal the deeper problem teach the reader to see what they're missing — and create the natural next step.

## Realistic posting cadence (revised in v1.2)

Given AI-assisted production, v1.2 sets a floor and a target rather than a fixed number. Floor is the minimum to maintain editorial momentum. Target is the stretch goal if review capacity holds.

| Series | Frequency | Floor (articles/year) | Target (articles/year) | Kagiso's review time per piece |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Search Pillar Pages | Monthly | 8 | 12 | 45-60 min |
| The Promotion Files | Monthly | 8 | 12 | 30-45 min |
| The Visibility Audit | Bi-weekly | 16 | 24 | 20-30 min |
| Skills That Pay | Quarterly | 4 (plus 4 satellites) | 4 (plus 8 satellites) | 60+ min per pillar piece |
| Manifesto pieces | Every 6-8 weeks | 4 | 6-8 | 30-45 min |

**Floor total:** 40 articles/year (~3.3 per month)
**Target total:** 50-60 articles/year (~4-5 per month)
**Kagiso review time at floor:** 25-30 hours/year (~30-35 min/week averaged)
**Kagiso review time at target:** 35-45 hours/year (~40-55 min/week averaged)

The floor is what she should be hitting regardless of capacity issues. The target is what to push for if data shows the review pace doesn't degrade quality. Don't lock in the lower number prematurely. Three months in, evaluate the actual review experience and decide which side of the range to settle into.

## Connection to the Video Delivery Playbook (NEW in v1.2)

Every launch article must have a video repurposing plan documented at draft time. This turns six articles into 18-24+ social assets without extra strategic thinking. The Video Delivery Playbook provides the format guidance (hooks, visual moves, screen captures); this is where each article gets its specific repurposing assignment.

For each launch article, specify three things at draft time:

1. **One TikTok format** — talking head with text overlay, screen capture with voiceover, storytime, carousel, or reply-to-comment
2. **One LinkedIn format** — short text post pulling out the strongest line, carousel breaking down the framework, long-form repost, or document post
3. **One email angle** — what gets sent to the Brevo list when the article publishes; usually a short personal note from Kagiso with a "where this came from" framing and a link to the full piece

**Example: Launch Article #5 (LinkedIn Headline Mistake)**
- TikTok: Screen capture of a real (anonymised) LinkedIn headline being rewritten in real time, with the Recruiter Search Test as the framework callout
- LinkedIn: Carousel post showing 3-5 before/after headlines from the article, with a link to the full piece in the comments
- Email: Short note ("I rewrote a headline yesterday in 3 minutes. Here's what changed and why.") linking to the article and the LinkedIn Headline Swipe File

Each launch article in the lineup must have these three formats specified before it ships. The repurposing assignments live in the article's draft file as part of the metadata block. The AI builder pulls them into the social content production queue automatically.

## Connection to the masterclass funnel

The Insights page never says "and that's why you should book the masterclass." But the entire architecture moves readers toward it:

- **Pillar pages** drive search traffic into the funnel and convert via lead magnets, CV/LinkedIn services, and Career Clarity
- **Promotion Files** introduces the archetypes from the diagnostic and the frameworks Kagiso teaches in the masterclass
- **Visibility Audits** demonstrate her tactical eye, building trust for the masterclass deeper-work value
- **Skills That Pay** establishes her as the curator who knows what's worth learning
- **Manifesto pieces** establish the brand-level POV that differentiates her from other SA career coaches

The masterclass shows up explicitly only on its own page, in the navigation, and in newsletter emails dedicated to launch announcements. Never inside an Insights article.

## Appendix — questions for Kagiso before each article ships

For every article, before it goes live, Kagiso confirms:

1. Does the framework name in this article match how she'd actually name it in a session?
2. Is the example client story accurate to a real situation she's coached (with details anonymised)?
3. Does the closing CTA point to the right service or lead magnet?
4. Is there anything in the piece that contradicts the brand firewall (employer, AIISA, banking, insurance, financial services)?
5. Does it sound like her, read out loud?

If any answer is no, revise. If all five pass, ship.
